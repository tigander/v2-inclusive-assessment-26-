CULTURALLY RESPONSIVE PEDAGOGIES / OVERVIEW
Seven principles to effectively support Maori students as Maori

--- OVERVIEW ---
Research suggests that the cultural gap between mainstream values, as represented by the education system, and Maori cultural values is the primary factor related to Maori underachievement.

The guide lists seven approaches that research has shown make a difference for Maori students' learning, achievement, and wellbeing:
1. Accept professional responsibility for, and make a commitment to, improving Maori students' educational achievement.
2. Care for Maori students as Maori.
3. Develop relationships with whanau and iwi.
4. Transform power relations in the classroom.
5. Develop discursive and co-constructive pedagogies.
6. Manage classrooms to promote learning.
7. Have high expectations of Maori students, and reflect on learning outcomes and goals with students and whanau.

--- CARING FOR MAORI STUDENTS AS MAORI ---
Relationships are key to the effective engagement of Maori students in education. Quality teacher-student relationships are strongly associated with academic achievement.

Maori students want teachers who are friendly, helpful and positive toward them. They want teachers to know them, their families, and their contexts well. They seek whanau-type relationships created within connection and shared experience of working together in the group.

What this looks like:
- Learning to pronounce students' names accurately
- Treating students and their whanau with respect
- Making an effort to get to know students well
- Trying to understand students' worlds as Maori and as young people
- Affirming students' ability
- Ensuring learning environments are secure and fair
- Extending knowledge and use of te reo Maori and tikanga Maori
- Including Maori materials and contexts in curriculum content and displays

--- DEVELOP RELATIONSHIPS WITH WHANAU AND IWI ---
Building strong relationships with parents, whanau and iwi and strengthening their engagement in students' learning can improve students' achievement.

What this looks like:
- Build meaningful, respectful partnerships focused on students
- Let whanau determine how they will participate
- Get to know the school's whanau personally, not only professionally
- Try different ways of communicating with whanau
- Be seen at community functions and connect with local iwi
- Work with whanau to explore students' potential and align school and whanau aspirations
- Invite families and iwi to contribute expertise and local knowledge
- Provide physical and cultural spaces in school for whanau
- Provide comprehensive information about learning and enough time to discuss it

--- TRANSFORM POWER RELATIONS IN THE CLASSROOM ---
Being culturally responsive to Maori students involves developing educational relationships founded on rangatiratanga or self-determination. This involves restructuring power relationships in the classroom so students can be autonomous and contribute from a position of self-determination.

What this looks like:
- Encourage students to initiate interactions
- Create relationships in which Maori students can participate on their own terms
- Involve students in curriculum decision-making
- Share power over curriculum content and learning direction
- Collaborate in critical reflection on classroom power relationships

--- DEVELOP DISCURSIVE AND CO-CONSTRUCTIVE PEDAGOGIES ---
Culturally responsive approaches require learning relationships and interactions which enable Maori ways of learning. The guide highlights three concepts:
- Wananga: rich and dynamic building and sharing of knowledge
- Ako: teachers and students learn from each other
- Whanau: connectedness, mutual respect, and commitment to the group

The guide states that teaching should reduce transmission approaches and whole-class teaching and instead use collaborative approaches based on:
- Student-generated questions
- Brainstorming
- Informal or structured group activities
- Inquiry or project-based work
- Co-constructing assessment tasks and assessment criteria

It explicitly notes that these practices are effective not only for Maori students but for all students.

--- MANAGE CLASSROOMS TO PROMOTE LEARNING ---
Effective interactions for learning depend on well-managed, well-organised, and secure environments that create safe spaces for each student to contribute to learning and support others' learning.

What this looks like:
- Carefully planned lessons with clear links between goals and activities
- Multiple task contexts and opportunities to adjust learning direction
- Effective monitoring of individual and group work
- Timely feed-forward and feedback
- Non-confrontational classroom management
- An organised classroom that students can use independently

--- HAVE HIGH EXPECTATIONS OF MAORI STUDENTS ---
Shared and supported high aspirations are crucial for raising achievement. Maori students appreciate clear teaching and learning goals, negotiated with the teacher, and an understanding of what meeting those goals involves.

What this looks like:
- Set positive academic goals together with students
- Express high expectations while supporting and rewarding effort
- Help students understand achievement and next learning steps
- Use evidence of progress to inform and transform teaching
- Approach struggling students privately and supportively to avoid shame or whakama

--- WHAT'S YOUR FIRST STEP? ---
The guide suggests collaboratively developing plans and strategies to promote:
- Non-dominating and interdependent relations in the classroom
- Discursive interactions and caring relationships
- Dialogic, interactive and collaborative modes of learning
- A culture of mutual support toward a common vision of excellence in outcomes

Use formal and informal monitoring and evaluation of Maori students' progress to inform practice and plan for change.
